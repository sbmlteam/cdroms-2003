Hi Joerg,

(did you get your memo-pad? ;)

> > (1) In reality it is a natural number in the cell.     It is exact, and is better to 
> >       support 'exact' methods such as Gillespie. 
> 
> Most models concern not one specific cell, but the "average" cell. In 
> this case even fractional molecule counts make sense.

I agree that in practice it is useful to allow real numbers as averages in a population.   

But mathematically it is problematic unless the model is completely linear.  
What if there are at least two groups in the population which have different
'average' concentrations of a specie, say, 10mM and 20mM.    The average over
the whole population, 15mM yields completely different simulation result from the reality.
Anyways I agree it is necessary in practice.


> > (2) 'substance' is the primary representation, and concentration is secondary.
> >       This can be understood by the fact that the simplest way to have support for changing
> >       volume is to store the value as 'substance', not 'concentration'.
> > 
> > The input/output of the simulator can either be concentration or the exact number, 
> > but the better internal representation by multi-compartment simulation is not
> > concentration.    
> > 
> 
> Remember that SBML is a standard for the exchange of models, not for the 
> internal representation of the model. This point was made several times 
> at the Florida meeting. 

It is what I have been saying too!

But still I think the whole (at least computational cell biology)
community is moving toward counts rather than concentration as the
stochastic simulation methods are being accepted.


-Kouichi
