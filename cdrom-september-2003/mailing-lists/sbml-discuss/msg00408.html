On Tuesday 03 June 2003 08:57, hugh.d.spence@gsk.com wrote:
> - a reaction has a place in the diagram (this place could be the center of
>
> the reaction, the place where the arrows meet)
>         As Herbert said, not necessarily at the center, but the
> intersection of the arrows.

Ok. You are right. For me the center of a reaction is the place where the arcs 
meet.

> - the graphical representation of a speciesreference is a line or curve in
> the diagram leading from a reaction center to a graphical representation
> of a species.
>
>         I think of the reaction as the sum of the center and the arrows.
> The speciesreference is merely an instance of the species. As the arrow
> have directionality (which is an important part of the reaction) and
> speciesreferences don't, mapping one on top of the other seems like a bad
> idea to me.
>

The speciesreference is the object in the reaction that says which species 
take part in the reaction. The arrows in the diagram are the objects that 
connect a reaction center to a graphical representaiton of a species.
In the same way as the graphical representation of a reaction is the sum of 
the center and the arrows, the reaction itself is the sum of the name, 
kinetic function and all the speciesreferences. That makes it natural to map 
the arrows (or arcs) to the species references. I do not see how an arrow 
would make sense that could not be attributed to exactly one 
speciesreference.
And a species reference has a direction since it is in the list of reactants 
or in the list of products.
