As I understand it--and I'm not a biologist or biochemist--your first example:

> The rate law:
> 
> k1*X - k2*X^2 
> 
> flips behaviour.
> 
> The species X behaves as an activator at low concentrations and an inhibitor
> at high concentrations. This rate law has been used in the past to model
> positive feedback.

is complicated because it is an abstract model which aggregates at least two 
distinct underlying mechanisms, which in combination produce the 
non-monotonic rate law. In many contexts, this abstraction/aggregation is 
both entirely adequate and more accurately reflects the current level of 
actual knowledge.

In other contexts--in particular, when attempting to model the behavior of the 
system under conditions which are intended or expected to differentially 
impact the distinct component mechanisms underlying that aggregate rate 
law--such abstract models will not be adequate. In such contexts, such models 
will have to be decomposed into their constituent sub-processes, each of 
which is (by definition) a distinct monotonic component. That is what I meant 
by a low-level/granular model.


Of course, your second example (pH) is non-monotonic for a different reason; 
it is not an aggregate process. So perhaps almost all actual models will end 
up including at least some non-monotonic rate laws, no matter how 
low-level/granular they are. But I don't know, that's the question I'm 
posing.
