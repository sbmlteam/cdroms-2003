To address the second issue, we are more interested in steady-state flux 
balance models of metabolism, which is still quantitative in the sense 
that we
use the matrix of stoichiometric coefficients to solve for a feasible 
space of allowable fluxes, but qualitative in the sense that we model 
the effect of a gene
knockout on a reaction with boolean predicates.  I have already 
implemented a boolean predicate parser in python, but  I am very 
interested in the Prolog link you sent me, and have started hacking the 
example code in the tutorial.
I think this might be very helpful in representing the 
"gene-reaction-predicates" that we have defined for the purpose of 
determining the effect of a
gene knockout on a reaction.

> I agree with Herbert, but I see two distinct issues in what you describe:
> 1. You want to associate accession codes (and names,  etc. we call 
> this background information) to the species involved in the model.
>
> 2. You want to represent how species are produced by genes.
>
> I think that there are advantages in keeping the two issues separate.
>
> If you could represent how enzymes are assembled from gene products, 
> in a quantitative model, you could alter the rate of expression of a 
> particular gene (in the model), and simulate the effect of a knock-out.
>
> For instance, if gene A and gene B are needed to produce the active 
> enzyme, you may need:
>
> Gene-A -> transcript-A -> Protein-A (or skip middle step if the model 
> has no way to alter the
> amount of transcript-A)
> GeneB -> transcript-B ->Protein-B
>
> (Don't forget degradation: )
> Protein-A > X
> Protein-B > X
>
> Protein-A+Protein-B <-> A-B-enzyme
>
> S <A-B-enzyme> P
>
> This is similar to what Herbert Sauro suggested. If you do not have 
> kinetic parameters
> for the various transitions, but still want to predict what reactions 
> are affected when you
> knock out a gene, you need another approach for "knowledge 
> representation".
> If would suggest looking into Prolog (see 
> http://www.csupomona.edu/~jrfisher/www/prolog_tutorial/intro.html for 
> an introduction)
> because this would let you express easily that a reaction cannot occur 
> if any of its substrate
> has been knocked out, or is the product of a KO gene.
>
> The first problem is related to issues that we have started to address 
> with the development
> of SigPath (http://www.sigpath.org). Perhaps you could find the system 
> useful if one of your
> goals is to annotate pathways with links to proteins and genes. 
> (SigPath can export
> quantitative models to SBML, at the moment qualitative pathways cannot 
> be exported,
> but this could be done if it was useful for specific applications, 
> such as yours perhaps).

>>   For the models I am creating, the number of reactions runs into the 
>> hundreds, and each reaction may be catalyzed by one or more enzymes, 
>> each of which can be composed of one or more gene products.
>> The problem comes from trying to distinguish between 2 genes whose 
>> products form a complex, and 2 genes whose products  are isoenzymes 
>> of a reaction.
>> In the first case, you knock out one gene, you knock out the 
>> reaction. In the second case, you must knock out both genes to knock 
>> out the reaction.  I do not believe the current implementation of 
>> modifier species
>> allows you to distinguish between these cases.  To illustrate what I 
>> am talking about, for the reaction I am interested in (EC# 6.4.2.1), 
>> there are two enzyme which catalyze it:
>>
>> <reaction id="ACETYL-COA-CARBOXYLTRANSFER-RXN" name="EC# 6.4.1.2" 
>> reversible="true" >
>>
>> <listOfModifiers>
>>
>> <modifierSpeciesReference species="ACETYL-COA-CARBOXYLTRANSFER-CPLX"/>
>>
>> <modifierSpeciesReference species="ACETYL-COA-CARBOXYMULTI-CPLX"/>
>>
>> </listOfModifiers>
>> </reaction>
>>
>> In this case, acetyl-coa-carboxyltransfer-cplx and 
>> acetyl-coa-carboxymulti-cplx are isoenzymes of the reaction.
