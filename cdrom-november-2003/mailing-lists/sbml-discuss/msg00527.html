Hi Hugh--
> We ALREADY have the modifier tag, without qualifiers in level 2.
> 
> So we HAVE to examine the math (and hope the SBML has treated it 
> properly) to find out whether we are looking at an inhibitor, an 
> activator, or a catalyst.
> 
> 
> If the model includes transcription of the enzyme and degradation of 
> it over time, we have to take the concentration of the enzyme into 
> account in our equations.
> 
> Inhibitors and activators have no effect in the absence of the enzyme.
> 
> SBML level 2 allows kineticLaw to be optional. In the absence of a
> kineticLaw, knowing that a species is a modifier, without knowing
whether it
> is essential to the reaction taking place or merely modifies it's
rate, means
> that the tag is practically meaningless.
> 
The modifierSpeciesReference is devoid of content beyond its ID, and
devoid of 
effect in the absence of its use in a kineticLaw. Thus it is true that
the tag 
is in this case meaningless, but this is not a failing of the SBML
design; it is 
a failing of the model as specified, hence of the modeller (or--if 
machine-generated--the designer of the software which generated the
model).

In such degenerate cases, adding catalyst or inhibitor or cofactor or
other tags 
does not help. There is no principled distinction between a catalyst and
an 
activator; the effect of each is to modify the rate. That's why I tend
to think 
of these categories as rather arbitrary judgments whose focus is more
aesthetic 
than substantive, and why they appear to me to belong in the
presentation rather 
than the model.

After all, in the nondegenerate cases which include both modifiers and 
references to them in the kineticLaw, if I tag the modifier as
"inhibitory" but 
the math says it's increasing the rate, who are you gonna believe?
