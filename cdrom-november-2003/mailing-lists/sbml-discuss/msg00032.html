On Wed, 18 Dec 2002, temporary user wrote:

> I would like to have the opinion of the members of the mailing list on the
> question of patented, trademarked, or copyrighted work. What level of
> freedom is expected in the licence agreement before such a work may be
> used as a standard, such as SBML? In particular, must the technology be
> licenced royalty free to all users, or is it sufficient to guarantee that
> it is and will be free for academic and non-profit users?
> 
> I work for Gene Network Sciences, Inc., and we have a patent pending on
> the Diagrammatic Cell Language for building models. This language
> works well as a description language, and it could be a useful standard
> for the network diagrams that accompany model descriptions, but we must
> know how free it has to be.

That is an interesting question, actually quite complex. Basically, you
cannot protect a language with a patent. The patent protects the NAME of
the language. The grammar of a language continuously evolving, you would
need a new patent for each version ... So if the community is really
interested in your language, but cannot use it freely, it will just
modify it a little bit and rename it. End of the story. This is not what
you want, and your interest is to open as much as possible the access to
the language specs. Afterward, you can make money on the
applications. The best example here is probably Adobe (think also of
Sun).

Now, you have to distinguish between "free" in the sens of "free beer"
and "free" as in freedom. If you want your language to be used, you have
to make it free according to the second meaning. Here are some example
of "free" licences for languages.

Python 2.0.1 license
  http://www.python.org/2.0.1/license.html

Artistic license 2.0
  http://dev.perl.org/rfc/346.html

You can then make money on distribution of libs, progs and docs.
