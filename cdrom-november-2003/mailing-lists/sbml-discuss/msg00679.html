> A modifier should be a substance whose concentration affects 
> the rate of reaction but not is not itself transformed after 
> a full reaction turnover. This should be defined in terms of 
> the rate law, not what it binds or not physically. In fact 
> the term modifier always relates to a rate law, not to the 
> reaction. In that sense if the reaction is represented at the 
> level of elementaryt steps then there is no modifier, as all 
> the rate laws would be of mass action type (thought a 
> modifier of the enzymatic rate law would be a susbtrate and 
> product of at least two of these elementary steps).
> 
> I hope that SBML will not go against the well-defined use of the term.
> 
> Also, relating to HerbertÂ¥s comment about the fact that non 
> lazy programs can discover modifiers in the rate laws. Indeed 
> this is the case, but why force so much computation and 
> programming when it is simple to *write* out the list of 
> modifiers versus *reading* it in in that cumbursome way? 
> Having an explicit list will also allow algorithms that have 
> no knowledge about rate laws to use that information (and I 
> do not know what these would be! but doing this keeps 
> flexibility in the spec allowing for future uses of SBML)
