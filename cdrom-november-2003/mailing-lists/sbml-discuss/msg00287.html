Ok I didn't say fractal dimension is necessary
for SBML, though I said it is nice to have.

HOWEVER:

Fractal dimension is a measurable physical quantity.    How can a surface area,
for example, be calculated from a volume without that?   Yes, we can always define
volume and area separately, but it is somewhat the same situation as the 
'amount/concentration/volume' problem.   Specifying two of the three is enough, and necessary.

So this is a design decision.

And I admit allowing fractal dimension at this time can
complicate the whole thing in most use cases.


> What is the volume of a compartment with fractal dimension?
>  From what I understand about fractals, this is not defined. You can 
> define the volume occupied in the space where the fractal is embedded 
> (say in d-dimensional space), and this changes as you look at different 
> refinement levels (One posible definition of fractal dimension). Or you 
> can talk about the extend in one dimension lower (d-1), but this is 
> infinite.

By the way,

> Anyway, in nature there are no real fractals, all are multifractals 

There is, if wee see at the level where the compartment is defined, as Nicolas showed
us in his neuron example very nicely.   I agree all such are multifractals 
(in the sense you meant),  but to some extent it is true for
many physical parameters.
In astrophysics simulation a star is a particle, in MD atoms are.
